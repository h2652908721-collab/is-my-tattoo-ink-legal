🇦🇺 2026 UNSW study: every ink tested failed at least one EU safety limit

The ink in your tattoo may be banned in Europe. In Australia, no one tests it.

A 2026 UNSW study analysed 15 tattoo inks sold in Australia — all from major international brands. Every single one would fail at least one EU safety rule. Here's what that means for your skin, and the questions to ask before your next session.

What the study actually found

Five plain-English facts

1

15 inks tested. All 15 failed. Every ink would fail at least one EU safety requirement.

2

Toxic metals over EU limits: arsenic, cadmium, chromium and lead were detected above EU thresholds.

3

Black inks carried the broadest range of regulated metals; bright colours carried high pigment-associated metals.

4

The EU has banned this since 2022 (REACH limits). Non-compliant inks can't legally be sold in the EU.

5

Australia has no equivalent rule. No national framework, no routine batch testing of tattoo inks.

The gap in one picture

Australia vs Europe

🇪🇺 European Union

Since 2022, binding chemical limits on tattoo inks (arsenic, cadmium, lead + carcinogenic organics). Inks that fail can't be sold. Artists must use compliant inks.

🇦🇺 Australia

No national tattoo-ink chemical standard. No requirement to batch-test. Oversight relies on voluntary compliance — the last government survey was back in 2016/2018.

Before you sit down

5 questions to ask your artist

  • ✓What's the exact brand and product name of the ink you'll use on me?
  • ✓Is this ink EU REACH compliant? (EU-compliant inks meet the strictest current limits.)
  • ✓Can I see the ink's safety data sheet (SDS) or ingredient list?
  • ✓What's the batch number, and is it within its expiry?
  • ✓For colour work — do you have an EU-compliant alternative for the brightest pigments?

FAQ

Common questions

Does this mean my tattoo will give me cancer?

No — and the study did not claim that. Cancer Council Australia states tattoos have not been shown to cause cancer. The study only measured what's chemically in the inks, not health effects. It's about transparency and informed choice, not panic.

Which brands failed?

The study did not name specific brands — the researchers deliberately didn't disclose them, as batch surveillance is a regulator's job. So no honest tool can tell you "brand X is toxic." What we can tell you is what tends to show up by ink type, and the questions to ask.

So what can I actually do?

Ask the 5 questions above. EU REACH-compliant inks meet the strictest current limits, so asking whether an ink is EU-compliant is the single most useful question.
